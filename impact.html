What Olympia Unlocks for the Ethereum Classic Ecosystem

Olympia gives Ethereum Classic a protocol-native way to fund itself and a more modern fee model, without changing its fixed-emission schedule or Proof-of-Work consensus. This changes how clients are maintained, how infrastructure is funded, how builders plan long-term work, and how miners relate to network growth.

This page focuses on impact: what Olympia means in practice for different stakeholders and how it can be used to strengthen ETC’s long-term resilience.

Stakeholder Impact Overview

Olympia touches almost every part of the ecosystem, but in different ways. Some groups care most about fee mechanics, others about predictable funding or governance guarantees.

Clients Client Teams & Core Protocol Devs

For client maintainers and protocol developers, Olympia provides a credible, on-chain path to long-term funding of:

  • Core client maintenance and security patches.
  • New client implementations and diversification.
  • Research, audits, and protocol upgrades.

Instead of relying on sporadic grants or single-sponsor funding, teams can propose milestone-based roadmaps that the DAO funds directly from the treasury.

Infra Infrastructure & Public Goods

RPC providers, explorers, indexers, testnets, and other shared services can request stable, multi-quarter support in exchange for SLAs, transparency, and uptime.

  • Keep critical services online even in bear markets.
  • Fund testnets, dev tools, and monitoring.
  • Support cross-chain bridges and oracles on ETC.

This reduces the risk that key infrastructure quietly disappears when external sponsors change priorities.

Builders dApp & Ecosystem Projects

Developers building on ETC gain access to transparent, on-chain funding for:

  • Early-stage experimentation and prototypes.
  • Production launches and growth initiatives.
  • Long-lived maintenance and community support.

Funding is competitive and reviewable. Proposals must justify why they create value for the network, and the DAO can prioritize public goods over short-lived promotions.

Miners Miners & Hashrate Providers

Miners keep the same fixed block rewards and earn priority fees. BASEFEE is redirected to a shared treasury, potentially smoothed by ECIP-1115.

  • Long-term security budget benefits as network usage grows.
  • Potential for dedicated “miner-focused” funding streams (e.g., firmware, pool tools).
  • Stronger ecosystem = more transactions = more fee revenue overall.

Miners are key stakeholders in discussions about fee parameters and smoothing behavior.

Users End Users & Holders

For everyday users and long-term ETC holders, Olympia aims to:

  • Improve fee predictability via BASEFEE and EIP-1559-style mechanics.
  • Reduce ecosystem fragility by ensuring critical software is funded.
  • Make treasury usage visible, auditable, and openly debated.

The goal is a more reliable network that doesn’t depend on any single donor or company.

Donors External Donors & Partners

Institutions or individuals who want to support ETC can:

  • Send contributions directly to the treasury address.
  • Co-fund specific proposals alongside protocol revenue.
  • Observe exactly how their contributions are used on-chain.

Olympia creates a neutral, shared funding venue that outlives any single organization.

Concrete Use Cases

The Olympia framework is intentionally general-purpose. Here are some concrete ways it can be used in practice across the Ethereum Classic ecosystem.

Use Case Multi-Client Maintenance

ETC’s security depends on having multiple, well-maintained clients. Olympia can fund:

  • Core maintenance for at least two independent clients.
  • Bug bounties, audits, and hard-fork coordination work.
  • New implementation efforts to diversify the client set.

Use Case Infrastructure Reliability

The DAO can allocate recurring budgets to:

  • Block explorers and transaction analytics.
  • RPC gateways and archival nodes.
  • Monitoring, alerting, and incident response tooling.

In return, these providers publish uptime reports and commit to clear SLAs.

Use Case Grants & Ecosystem Growth

Builders can propose funding for:

  • New DeFi, NFT, or infrastructure dApps on ETC.
  • Educational content, documentation, and onboarding programs.
  • Hackathons, incubators, and region-specific community efforts.

Milestone-based disbursements help ensure funds are tied to real progress.

Use Case Risk & Security Programs

Olympia can support a standing security budget for:

  • Independent audits of critical contracts and protocols.
  • Formal verification work on client code or core contracts.
  • Incident response playbooks and simulations.

Over time, the DAO can build a track record of proactive security investment.

Use Case Child-DAOs & Specialization

The main Olympia DAO can route funds into Child-DAOs focused on:

  • Core protocol & client funding.
  • Infrastructure and tooling.
  • Ecosystem grants and community programs.

Each Child-DAO can adopt governance structures best suited for its domain, while the main treasury remains centralized and auditable.

Use Case Co-Funding with External Partners

External organizations can co-sponsor proposals that:

  • Match protocol funding with off-chain contributions.
  • Target shared priorities like client robustness or specific integrations.
  • Leverage the DAO’s transparency and accountability mechanisms.

This turns Olympia into a coordination hub rather than a closed grants program.

Neutral Infrastructure: Olympia doesn’t dictate which projects “must” be funded. It provides a structured, transparent process so that competing visions can be debated in the open and decided through the DAO.

Risks, Tradeoffs & Constraints

Olympia is designed to solve long-standing funding problems, but it introduces its own tradeoffs and new responsibilities for the ecosystem.

  • Governance quality matters: Having a treasury doesn’t guarantee that funds are used wisely. Poorly designed processes or apathy can lead to waste.
  • Coordination overhead: Structured funding requires documentation, review, and monitoring. This is more work than ad-hoc grants, but also more accountable.
  • Incentive balance: BASEFEE redirection changes the balance between miner revenue and protocol funding. Parameters like smoothing (ECIP-1115) must be tuned carefully.
  • Social resilience: Olympia cannot prevent every capture or conflict scenario. Its value depends on an active, informed community willing to engage.
Guardrails, not Guarantees: The ECIPs provide structural guardrails— immutability, non-inflation, timelocks, transparency—but outcomes depend on how the ecosystem chooses to use these tools.

Measuring Impact Over Time

Olympia’s success should be judged on observable outcomes, not just design intent. Over time, the community can track metrics such as:

  • Client diversity: Number of actively maintained ETC clients.
  • Infrastructure health: Uptime and redundancy of core services.
  • Developer activity: New repos, commits, and deployed dApps on ETC.
  • Treasury flows: Inflows from BASEFEE and outflows by category.
  • Governance reliability: Proposal throughput, execution success rate, and incident history.

Dashboards and explorers can make these metrics visible, helping ETC stakeholders evaluate whether Olympia is delivering on its goals and where adjustments are needed.
