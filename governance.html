ECIP-1113 & ECIP-1114 · Governance & Funding Process

Modular DAO Governance for a Protocol-Native Treasury

ECIP-1113 and ECIP-1114 define the Olympia DAO—a modular governance framework and funding proposal process that controls how ETC from the Olympia Treasury is allocated. The treasury itself (ECIP-1112) stays immutable and minimal; all governance logic lives in separate, upgradeable DAO modules.

These ECIPs focus on mechanics, not voters. They define how proposals move, how votes are tallied, and how funds are executed, while leaving identity, sybil resistance, and delegate selection to the social layer and future Olympia Improvement Proposals (OIPs).

Governance Architecture: How the Pieces Fit

Olympia governance is built as a layer around the immutable treasury. Rather than embedding complex rules directly into the vault, ECIP-1113 defines a system of modules and executors that interact with the treasury via a narrow, well-defined interface.

At a high level, the architecture includes:

  • Treasury Vault (ECIP-1112) – Holds ETC and enforces basic invariants.
  • Proposal Module – Manages creation, states, and metadata of proposals.
  • Voting Module(s) – Collect and tally votes according to a chosen model.
  • Executor / Timelock – Queues and executes approved actions after delay.
  • Child-DAOs – Optional sub-treasuries or domains with specialized rules.
DAO as an interface, not a monolith Treasury invariants stay fixed Governance modules can evolve Supports multiple voting models
Key Idea: The treasury does not know how voting works. It only trusts specific executor contracts. Governance modules handle all the complexity and can be refined over time without touching the vault.

Governance Modules (ECIP-1113)

ECIP-1113 describes the Olympia DAO in terms of composable modules. This keeps the framework flexible enough to support different eras of governance, while anchoring all flows to a single protocol treasury.

Module Type Proposal Module

The proposal module is responsible for proposal lifecycle management. It defines how proposals are created, which fields they must include, and how they move between states (e.g., Draft → Active → Succeeded → Queued → Executed).

Module Type Voting Module

Voting modules handle how votes are recorded and evaluated. ECIP-1113 does not prescribe a single model—coin-weighted, identity-based, delegated, or hybrid systems can all be implemented as modules.

Module Type Executor & Timelock

Once a proposal passes, an executor module queues its actions behind a timelock. After the delay, the executor can call the treasury vault and other contracts to carry out the approved changes.

Module Type Child-DAOs & Routers

ECIP-1113 supports Child-DAOs—sub-governance domains that can manage specific streams of funding (e.g., client maintenance, infrastructure, ecosystem grants).

Modularity in Practice: The network can start with a conservative, simple configuration and later add or swap modules through governance, without redeploying the treasury or rewriting ECIP-1112.

Proposal Lifecycle & Funding Process (ECIP-1114)

ECIP-1114 describes the funding proposal process—the path a proposal takes from initial idea to executed payment. It standardizes the states, required metadata, and execution rules so that every proposal is auditable and machine-readable.

1. Creation & Metadata

  • Requested ETC amount and payment schedule (e.g., milestones).
  • Target addresses (recipients, escrow contracts, or Child-DAOs).
  • Links to off-chain metadata (specs, milestones, docs, audits).
  • Optional category tags (core client, infra, R&D, ecosystem, etc.).

2. Review & Activation

Proposals may go through a review window before voting begins, allowing technical review and community feedback before the proposal becomes Active.

3. Voting & Resolution

During the voting period, the configured voting module:

  • Collects support/against/abstain votes according to its model.
  • Enforces quorum, thresholds, and any special conditions.
  • Reports the final outcome back to the proposal module.

4. Timelock & Execution

Successful proposals are queued in an execution timelock. After the delay:

  • The executor contract reads the instructions embedded in the proposal.
  • Calls the treasury and other contracts to carry out payments or configuration changes.

5. Milestone-Based Disbursements

ECIP-1114 supports milestone-based funding, where a total requested amount is split into multiple tranches tied to progress or deliverables.

Transparency by Default: Because all proposals and their executions are on-chain, explorers and dashboards can show exactly where treasury funds are going, and which DAO decisions authorized them.

Identity, Sybil Resistance & Participation

A crucial design choice in ECIP-1113/1114 is what they do not do: they do not hard-code any specific identity or voting scheme into the protocol.

Instead, they:

  • Define interfaces for voting modules, not the internals.
  • Allow multiple models (token-based, identity-based, delegation, reputation) to coexist over time.
  • Treat identity and sybil resistance as social-layer problems to be solved via future Olympia Improvement Proposals (OIPs) and off-chain coordination.
No governance token required at launch Supports future identity & delegation schemes Protocol remains governance-neutral

This keeps Ethereum Classic’s base protocol neutral while still enabling experimentation and iteration in the DAO layer. Governance can evolve without reopening the ECIP-1112 vault or touching core consensus rules.

FAQ – Olympia Governance

Who gets to vote in the Olympia DAO?

ECIP-1113/1114 intentionally do not specify a single voter set. They provide the framework for voting modules; the actual choice of identity system and eligibility rules is made by the community through social consensus and future OIPs.

Is there a governance token?

The Olympia ECIPs do not require a governance token at launch. A token model could be proposed later as a separate OIP/ECIP, but the framework is compatible with non-token approaches as well (e.g., identity-based or multi-stakeholder models).

Can governance modules be upgraded?

Yes. Governance contracts (proposal, voting, executors) are designed to be upgradeable or replaceable via the DAO itself, within rules set in ECIP-1113/1114. The treasury contract (ECIP-1112) remains immutable, protecting funds from arbitrary changes.

What happens if governance behaves poorly or is captured?

The ECIPs cannot eliminate social risk, but they:

  • Enforce timelocks, providing time to observe and respond to decisions.
  • Keep the treasury immutable, avoiding backdoor minting or upgrades.
  • Make all decisions fully transparent and traceable on-chain.

How can I follow or participate in governance?

Participation will be coordinated through public ETC channels—Discord, GitHub, community calls, and future governance portals. On-chain, you’ll be able to:

  • Inspect proposals and their metadata.
  • See how your chosen voting module represents your preferences.
  • Track execution of approved proposals through the treasury.
