ECIP-1112 · Protocol-Controlled Treasury Contract

A Non-Inflationary, Immutable Vault for Protocol Revenue

ECIP-1112 defines the Olympia Treasury: a protocol-controlled, immutable smart contract that receives BASEFEE redirected by ECIP-1111 and disburses funds only through standardized governance calls defined in ECIP-1113 and ECIP-1114.

The treasury does not mint new ETC, has no privileged upgrade keys, and is intentionally minimalist. Its purpose is to securely hold protocol revenue and execute payments authorized by the Olympia DAO.

Role of the Olympia Treasury

The Olympia Treasury is the on-chain vault at the center of the upgrade. It is the endpoint for redirected BASEFEE and the source of funds for approved public-goods proposals.

In the Olympia architecture:

  • ECIP-1111 handles BASEFEE accounting and redirection.
  • ECIP-1112 defines the treasury vault that receives those funds.
  • ECIP-1113 defines the DAO framework that can authorize withdrawals.
  • ECIP-1114 defines the proposal and execution process.

You can think of the treasury as:

Single source of truth for protocol funds Minimal, auditable state machine No governance baked into the vault Governed externally by Olympia DAO

The contract itself does not decide who gets funded or why. It only enforces what is allowed to happen with the funds based on calls that match the rules in the ECIP-1112 specification.

Design & Core Invariants

ECIP-1112 is built around a small set of strict invariants. These invariants are what give the treasury its security properties and make it acceptable as a protocol-level funding mechanism.

Invariant 1 No Minting, No Inflation

The treasury cannot mint ETC. It can only hold what it receives (e.g., redirected BASEFEE) and send those funds to other addresses. The ETC supply curve defined by Ethereum Classic’s fixed emission remains untouched.

  • No mint() or similar functions.
  • No ability to alter block rewards or schedule.
  • All inflows come from existing protocol revenue streams.

Invariant 2 Immutable & Protocol-Controlled

Once deployed and activated by consensus, the treasury contract is non-upgradable. There are no admin keys, no proxy patterns, and no privileged “owner” role.

  • Contract code is finalized at deployment.
  • No external authority can change its logic.
  • Control flows entirely from protocol rules + DAO calls.

Invariant 3 Restricted Write Access

Only a narrow set of callers are allowed to instruct the treasury to send funds: authorized executor contracts defined in ECIP-1113/1114.

  • No arbitrary external accounts can withdraw directly.
  • Executors must conform to a specified interface.
  • DAO modules can be upgraded, but the vault rules stay fixed.

Invariant 4 Transparent, Read-Only Views

Anyone can query the treasury’s balance and relevant metadata. This makes the protocol’s funding state fully observable and auditable by explorers, dashboards, and the community.

  • Simple read-only methods expose balances & payment history.
  • Supports analytics and monitoring without special access.
Separation of Concerns: ECIP-1112 treats the treasury as a pure vault. All politics and preferences live in the governance layer (ECIP-1113/1114), not in the contract that holds funds.

Funding Flows: From Fees to Public Goods

The Olympia Treasury exists as part of a wider pipeline that connects transaction fees to public-goods funding. At a high level, the flow looks like this:

  1. Fees Paid: Users submit transactions using EIP-1559-style fields (ECIP-1111), paying BASEFEE + tip.
  2. Revenue Split: Miners receive block rewards and tips, while BASEFEE is redirected to the treasury contract (once ECIP-1112 is active).
  3. Treasury Accumulation: Over time, the treasury accumulates ETC from BASEFEE inflows, optionally modulated by the smoothing layer in ECIP-1115.
  4. DAO Decisions: Proposals in the Olympia DAO (ECIP-1113/1114) reach an approved state after voting and timelocks.
  5. Authorized Execution: A DAO executor contract calls the treasury according to the ECIP-1112 interface to release funds to recipients or milestone contracts.

At every step, the treasury’s behavior is deterministic and auditable. It does not know about “projects” or “milestones” by itself; it only executes calls that satisfy the conditions in its specification.

ECIP-1115 Integration: If the network adopts smoothing, the treasury may interact with a smoothing mechanism that controls how quickly BASEFEE-derived value is made available for spending—without altering the treasury’s core invariants.

Security, Auditability & Client Integration

Because the treasury holds protocol-level funds, its security properties must be boringly robust. ECIP-1112 is written with a conservative design philosophy:

  • Minimal attack surface: No complex business logic inside the vault.
  • Explicit invariants: Clear statements about what can and cannot happen.
  • Formal & community review: Open to external audits and formal verification.
  • Client neutrality: The contract address and interface are treated as consensus parameters shared by all ETC clients.

Node operators and client implementers only need to:

  • Ensure ECIP-1111 correctly redirects BASEFEE to the treasury address.
  • Respect the treasury’s existence as part of consensus state (like any other contract).
  • Expose RPC and tracing tools that make treasury interactions easy to monitor.
No Special Privileges for Clients: The treasury is a normal smart contract from the client’s perspective. Its “specialness” comes from consensus agreement, not hidden logic or client-specific shortcuts.

FAQ – Olympia Treasury

Does the treasury change ETC’s monetary policy?

No. The treasury does not mint coins. It only receives existing protocol revenue (BASEFEE) and disburses ETC that already exists. Block rewards and the emission schedule remain unchanged.

Who controls the treasury?

No individual or foundation controls the treasury. It is controlled by on-chain governance via the Olympia DAO contracts specified in ECIP-1113 and ECIP-1114. These DAO contracts, not humans with keys, authorize disbursements.

Can the treasury contract be upgraded or replaced?

The contract itself is non-upgradable. Replacing it would require a future network upgrade and new consensus decision, just like any other protocol change. Day-to-day governance operates around the immutable treasury, not inside it.

Can external donors send ETC to the treasury?

ECIP-1112 is compatible with additional inflows, such as community donations or institutional contributions, if the specification and implementation choose to expose that behavior. Any such design is intended to be transparent and auditable on-chain.

Where can I see how treasury funds are being used?

Because the treasury is a smart contract, all inflows and outflows are public. Explorers and dashboards can show:

  • Current treasury balance.
  • Historical inflows from BASEFEE.
  • Payments linked to approved proposals.
